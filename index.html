Personal Statement

In a windowless room deep within the coridors of the ED, only illuminated by the lights coming from the hallway, a patient looked at me in the eyes and said softly in Vietnamese that his goal for his emergency room visit visit was to stop drinking, and to tell the doctor to make sure the request goes through. I translate back what he said to the doctor, and after a quick examination, I follow the doctor out of the room. As my eyes are adjusting back to the full lights, the patient observers and nurses looked at us, expecting a debrief. "He was very kind to me," the doctor told the nurse. When the patient arrived earlier to the ED, he was causing a disturbance, being loud and slurring his words whenever he tried to speak - like a typical drunk patient who ends up in the ED. I had witnessed the interaction with the EMT and nurses earlier, and they had to call a phone translator, which probably did not help their patience with this Vietnamese-only speaking patient. Moreover, this patient presented to the ED often for being drunk. However, after our interaction with him, I never saw him again, in the remainder of the time I worked in that department. Perhaps he followed through his resolve that night, but the interaction left an impression on me as well: I don't think he would have looked at me in the eye and showed me such a vulnerable state had I not translated. If a phone interpreter had been used, he'd just speak into the phone, and the translator will respond back in a monotone voice with the words merely translated into English. Moreover, during our time in the room, he revealed that he is originally from Saigon, Vietnam, referring to modern-day Ho Chi Minh City as Saigon the same way my parents had when I was growing up, and that he does not have any other family in America, living alone on the 3rd floor of an apartment complex in Lynn, and that he was on social security disability. Even if this man and I had nothing else in common besides the fact that we were both born in Saigon and spoke Vietnamese, having a shared language where it is rarely spoken, seemed to connect us and have him be more pleasant and vulnerable to both the provider and I. Since this visit, I've had to translate several more times for Vietnamese-speaking patients for the doctors who I scribe for, and they have always been grateful for the care. Sometimes, I think their experience that night was different because they most likely receive care that is through a phone translator or very basic English, and that perhaps the doctor and I left a more positive impression. As a physician, I plan to continue connecting with Vietnamese patients by speaking their language, providing them comfort and memories of their home country, and learning more about them. Even for patients whose language I could not speak, I will learn about the medical care in their countries and ask about their life.

Change settings to a nursing home, with the chair angled next to the patient's bed, and the TV mounted high against the wall for the best viewing angle, and the TV contains a question asking about which celebrity has the first name of "Ladonna" and provided 3 multiple choices. The patient and I were torn between Madonna and Donna Summers, and the answer ended up being what we least expected: Donna Summers. We proceed to watch the contestant receive praise for answering correctly and then debate about the answer to the next question shown on the screen. In between commercial breaks, the hospice patient and I would update each other on any new developments within the past week. I'd always tell her it was the usual with studying and working, and then she'd update me with what she saw on her daily walks around the campus of the nursing home. After, she'd ask me about my dog and request that the dog come to visit again. Other times, the patient would have another family member in the room, and we'd all have great conversation about how their family doing. Whenever I'd try to take my leave, the patient expressed that I should stay a little longer, or visit her more often, and occassionally remark, "don't be a stranger." Every week, I look forward to my visits with this patient as a hospice volunteer. Sometimes, I felt that I should bring my dog because she used to have the same breed, a rough collie, named Teddy, and always recalled that they were wonderful and kind pets, and my dog hanging out in her room reminded her of Teddy. When I first became a hospice volunteer, I thought that I would just play games with patients and check in with them. I didn't expect to make another friend, get to know their family, and become a part of their life. At another nursing home, I had this patient with whom I first met and had difficulty speaking. Over the next few weeks playing games, drawing, and feeding her, her eyes would light up whenever I showed up. She soon started speaking in longer sentences, and the words coming out of her were more clear. Even when she was discharged from hospice services, I still set some time to see her, despite having to see 4 other patients, and even the staff noted that I become a friend to the patient. I enjoyed listening to the patients, learning about their life and family, and becoming a friend to them, and hope to continue this as a physician.

Prior to working as a scribe and hospice volunteer, at the beginning of college, I was an unremarkable student. Professors and TA would glare at me as I stepped through the doors for office hours, always visiting every week despite not having anything substantial to ask them. Looking back, I didn't learn any content or build on any questions from these office hour visits, as I should have reviewed the content and wrote out questions ahead of time. Needless to say, I didn't stand out from all the other 300 students my freshman year. When sophomore year came, I took the fall semester off and met a friend who was a popular iOS developer. Watching her remotely decrypt my broken iPhone via the command prompt in another state away, and the lines of codes executing successfully as she pressed the enter key, made me want to code just like her. The day after, I enrolled in a computer science course at the local community college. The professor who taught this class answered any and all questions ranging from "is 17.0 a double or integer" to "how do I get the app to close out after it reaches a certain value" without any judgement and encouraged the students to try writing code in different ways or just try and run a piece of code if there was a question in how it worked. For the first time in my college career, I read the textbooks before class, went to office hours with questions and built answers alongside the professor, and engaged with the content.

When I returned to my 4-year univeristy, I wanted to continue pursuing computer science, so I joined a computational biology research lab. On the first lab meeting of the semester, the PI asked me if I was premed. I answered no. The labwork was independent and involved me browsing through various genomics databases and transforming the data in Python. I was able to use coding beyond the confines of the classroom and apply it to a practical application. I also started becoming a regular to professor office hours. In particular, I went to a lot of literature and history office hours as I struggled to understand the reading, and thus, had a difficulty formulating an essay with a succint argument and sufficent evidence. Soon, I started going to these office hours just to talk about the content of the reading and recap on the discussions in class. These visits were lengthy, but a constrast to the girl who used to bother her professors. Little did I know at the time that I would develop a deep appreciation for the arts and literature, and this love for the humanities would translate into connecting with patients beyond the scientific aspect of medicine.

The same kid who spent time in her professors' office would soon find herself spending time in a physician's personal office. Whenever I reached out to a physician to shadow, they always wanted to meet with me in their office and discuss my background and future goals prior to the observation. In turn, I was able to ask the physician about their career as well. The most inspiring story was with Weiwei Shi, MD-PhD, as he was a cardiothoracic surgeon back in China, and came to America to do a PhD, but after the PhD, wanted to go back into medicine, and decided on pathology. Through this story, I realised that medicine was a calling, and it was worth the path, even if it meant restarting over. When I shadowed a pulmonary signout with him and Lynette Sholl, MD, we reviewed a lung biopsy showing fungus balls. She had Weiwei pull up the CXR for the patient and proceeded to ask me if I knew how to read the films. I told her no, but she jumped up and showed me the points of abnormality based on the discolouration. Her ethusiasm to teach was infectious, and I realised that a physician was not only a healer, but an educator. Scribing update notes for the patient as the physician tells the patient what to do after discharge, or watching the physician asking the patient if there are any additional questions, further reinforced me the role of a stellar physician in educating their patients. I wanted to be like Dr. Sholl and teach and inspire others as anyone I come across could be a colleague in the future.

While alone at a concert, during an intermisson with the stage crew replacing the keyboard on stage with a guitar, bass, and drum set, a woman appearing to be in her middle ages asked me what the concert T-shirt that I was holding in my hands looked like. We started to converse, and at one point, she told me about how she discovered the artist, Blu Eyes, 2 years ago while bedridden and recovering from her knee surgery. Blu Eye's music touched her deeply, and she became a fan ever since. Another girl at the concert, standing next to me, joined in on the conversation and said that Blu Eyes helped her make it through college. After a night of dancing and singing along, I kept replaying the interaction about how these two strangers shared Blu Eye's positive effects on their life. I realised that I wanted to have the same effect on people. A musical artist could have the same effect as a physician where they could positively impact someone's life. While I may have helped many of my hospice patients' last minutes on Earth, I still wanted to do more for their care than just conversing and playing games with them. As a physician, I want give all my time for the patient and make them feel heard and that they matter. Even if I see a patient briefly, I want to make a small, positive impact in their life, and have them reflect fondly of their memomry of me with positivity.

Through inspiration from other physicians, to personally connecting with patients and their families, to being encouraged and inspired by my professors, I was able to maximise the opportunities provided to me thus far. I want to give back by becoming through healing others and using my position as a physician to inspire future colleagues, students, and patients.

I wasn't a particularly ambitious kid growing up. I never had dreams to become a doctor. In fact, I felt discouraged to even pursue the career due to some uninspiring doctor's visits and unpleasant experiences with the members of the care team whenever I went to the hospital or emergency department. I was umambitious to the point that my dream job was to be an idol, and all I ever did back in high school was cross country and maintain my idol tumblr fanpages afterwards. Thus, when I entered college, I did poorly my first year. I didn't bother studying for classes and barely passed. Needless to say, I wasn't remarkable nor stood out in my 300-person lectures, and whenever I went to office hours, I didn't really ask anything about the course content and just bothered the professors and TAs, and it showed. The following school year, I decided to take a semester off. During my semester off, I met a friend who was an iOS developer, and when I watched her remotely decrypt my broken iPhone using the command prompt, I wanted to code like her and proceeded to enroll in my first computer science course at the local community college. The professor for the class encouraged the students to experiment with the code whenever they had a question or wanted to investigate how things worked further. I liked how in computer science, I was able to write a few lines of code and then run it to see the fruits of my labour, compared to a subject like biology where exams determined whether or not effort was put in. When I returned to college, I wanted to engage with computer science even further, so I joined the BINDs lab and worked on a project analysing the Gibbs Free Energy and the severity of different cancer types based on their protein-protein interaction networks. I attended my PI's classes where I learned about quantum computing, chaos computing, and graph theory which all furthered my interest in technology. I still remember in my very first lab meeting of the school year, the PI, Dr Edward Rietman, asked me if I was pre-med, and I answered "no."

I also became a regular at office hours for my elective humanities courses. I struggled a lot with formulating any clear argument backed up with reasonable evidence for the midterm papers in history and political science courses, since the content seemed to require more background knowledge than the required class readings provided, but at office hours, I'd bounce ideas with my professors and leave with more resources and feel more encouraged. Soon, when papers were not due, I'd still go to office hours and have lengthy discussions about the content covered in class with the professors. Initially, I was ashamed for enjoying my humanities courses more than my STEM courses because all the classmates in my major would complain about having to take such Gen Eds. Little did I know at the time, that a well-rounded physician not only excels at the sciences, but also engages with the arts and literature to further connect with their patients.

Fast forward to graduating college, where I did a summer internship at Harvey Mudd College to work on multi-compartmental model of T1DM. This project was my first insight into the new and rapidly evolving field of personalised medicine, as the project's goal was to create some fancy mathematical equations on T1DM and finding the best therapy for each patient depending on the severity of their disease. After this internship, I decided to transition out of dry-lab research and confine myself in the clinical microbiology lab of Brigham And Women's Hospital and run samples through a machine or read slides under a microscope to help doctors deduce what is wrong with their patient. Whenever I worked, I always wondered more: what was the patient presenting that warranted the doctor to order all these panels ? How is the doctor treating the patient after I communicated the results of the blood culture to them ? What is the patient's quality of life going to be like after their hospitalisation ? Initially, I asked the senior techs countless questions about the microbes, and they soon put in a good word for me to the clinical pathology residents. Because a pathologist is a not layperson's first thought of a doctor, and has even less prestige than the other non-patient facing dcotor, the radiologist, the pathology residents were enthused for a little disciple who showed interest. The first doctor I ever shadowed was James DiCarlo, MD-PhD, who loved the science behind medicine and not the patient interaction. Still, he encouraged me to further pursue the career and let me shadow a pulmonary pathology signout, attend pathology conferences, and introduced me to other members and parts of a pathology laboratory.

The pulmonary pathology signout was with Weiwei Shi MD-PhD, Lynette Sholl MD, and Lucian Chirieac MD. I recall Dr. Chirieac giving me a fist bump when he first stepped into the room and letting me ask a million questions after he finished reviewing the all the slides. After that, Dr. Sholl signed out her share of slides, and there was a particular slide suggesting fungus balls in a patient's lung biopsy. She had a her fellow pull up the CXR for the patient, and when she asked me if I knew how to read the films, I told her I didn't. That was when she jumped to her feet and started explaining how to look for abnormalities based on the discolouration and smoke in the film. Her passion for teaching was infectious, and that was when I realised that a physician was not only a healer, but also an educator. I was just a lowly tech who wasn't certified or trained in anything, yet she was enthused and took the time to teach. It reminded me of the time in my senior year of college, despite working for a research lab, I was still weak at reading journals, so I went to Professor Kroeger's office hours and asked her how to read a paper. She didn't put me down and explained each part of the paper and their purpose: the abstract, introduction, methods, results, and discussion, and what I should look for under each of them. I wanted to be like Dr. Sholl and Professor Kroeger and teach and inspire others as anyone I come across could be a colleague in the future.

Weiwei was a cardiothoracic surgeon back in China, but he came to America to do his PhD. After earning his PhD, he wanted to get back into medicine and figured that pathology would be a good fit. 2 years later, while I was riding my skateboard between parked cars and moving cars around a busy street at MGH, I started feeling knots in my right lower leg every time I pushed on my board. I had experienced this in the past, and it's usually due to overuse from doing tricks, and these knots will soon tighten and put my legs in a spasm. Just when I was about to ride through a crosswalk, the knots started becoming unbearable, suggesting that the long-dreaded spasm will inevitably begin, so I started to hold onto my right leg. A woman in a blue flannel shirt and jeans asked me if I was okay. I paused riding the skateboard and told her "no." My right leg then went into a temporary spasm, and she had me sit and began putting pressure on different parts of it and asking me if it hurt each time. She then told me to stay put, and that she'll be right back. Ten minutes go by, with people simply passing by me, showing that I'm in this large city, I am merely a background character of a film. Soon, the woman emerges out of MGH with a 1L SmartWater and a straw. She told me that she believes I went into a spasm because I was dehydrated due to not drinking enough water throughout the day, the humidity, and I was exercising. She offered to stay with me until I improved, but I told her I was feeling better and will rest for the remainder of the day. I also noticed that she had an "MD" title on her badge, and I asked her what type of doctor she was. She used to be a general practioner back in Iraq, but she was currently doing radiology research to be competitive enough to get a residency in the US. Weiwei and this lovely woman showed me that medicine is a calling, and they went after it even if it meant having to restart from the beginning.

Soon enough, I transitioned from the running lab tests to helping physicians with their notes down in the emergency department. My curiousity about presentation, differentials, and management were answered working as a scribe. The best part about the emergency department is that cases of all different pathologies are seen, which provides great learning opportunities for wondering young minds. I was fortunate enough to work with physicians who took the time to listen to the patient and go over their the plan of treatment and answer any questions. Countless patients have told the doctors that I worked with they deserve a doctor of the year award. I took the interactions I observed into my role as hospice volunteer, where I listened to the patient talk about their lives and build conversations from what they discuss with me. I also enjoyed speaking with the patient's care team about their progress and the team member's general life because a good physician knows all members of the team works together, and it's good to know them beyond their work as well.

After observing great physicians and having both professors and physicians let me see my potential and help me receive great opportunities, I want to give back to them through healing others and using my position to build a great team, exchange ideas with others, and mentor future colleagues.

  • Most Meaningful Essay: Flick the ankle against the back tail, jump up with the board, slide the front foot up towards the nose: those are the steps of an ollie. However, despite its simple description, it takes a long time to learn. After spending 4 hours at the basketball court repeatedly practicing ollies, I put on my jacket and was about to ride off until a man stopped me and insisted that he wanted to give me his deck. He then told me that my ollies would be higher if it was not for the razor-tail on my current deck and proceeds to transfer the parts on my current setup to that deck. For the next 2 years, I would learn how to ollie with the board he had given me, through many repeat attempts and feedback from other skateboarders. Even when I managed to do the ollie consistently, I still couldn't do them over or down anything and still needed to do them switch, fakie, and nollie. In skateboarding, there is always room for improvement. The board is a powerful tool that brings people together to accomplish a common goal with landing that next trick, and it is not able landing the flashiest tricks, but to stick with it, even when there doesn't seem to be any progress. Lastly, the sport led me to maintaining an active and healthy lifestyle, and I plan to continue with it to help me handle the rigors of medical school. (1312 characters)
